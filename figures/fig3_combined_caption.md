**Figure 3 | Training dynamics and physics signatures across six SFMs.** The training-time metric plotted in panels **a** and **b** is the symmetric InfoNCE batch-64 prediction accuracy averaged over both contrastive directions (agent → target and target → agent); random performance is 1/64 ≈ 1.6%, and absolute heights reflect both true model performance and the number of unique partners present per batch (eSFM is high because only 2,646 unique substrates exist, mhcSFM is low because 105 alleles are spread thinly across batches of 64 pairs). The retrieval metric in panel **c** is pool-512 R@1 (the post-hoc retrieval metric reported in Fig. 2). **(a)** Training (solid grey) and validation (dotted blue) batch prediction accuracy as a function of training budget (epoch / 100 × 100%) for each SFM, fold 0, identity_100 split. All six models were trained for 100 epochs with identical optimizer and scheduler settings (AdamW, lr = 10⁻³, CosineAnnealingWarmRestarts T₀ = 20, T_mult = 1, batch size 64). Open blue circles mark the epoch at which validation accuracy peaked; epoch numbers are annotated. Five of six SFMs reach peak validation within ~20% of the training budget — anomalously fast relative to standard supervised deep learning. tSFM peaks at epoch 52 (~50% of budget). The crisprSFM curves are taken from the surviving hard-negative-trained log; the corresponding base-training log was lost to scratch retention. **(b)** Validation-minus-training accuracy at the best-validation epoch. Positive bars (blue) indicate that validation exceeded training at the best checkpoint — an inverted train-test gap that runs counter to the conventional bias-variance description of generalization. Four of six SFMs show this inverted pattern (tSFM, mhcSFM, mir-SFM, dtSFM); eSFM is essentially zero (train and validation reach near-perfect accuracy together); only crisprSFM exhibits the standard pattern in which training exceeds validation. **(c)** Agent → target pool-512 R@1 as a function of out-of-distribution threshold stringency for four SFMs with multiple evaluated thresholds. Thresholds use MMseqs2 protein sequence identity (tSFM, eSFM, dtSFM) or Hamming nucleotide identity (crisprSFM). Filled markers indicate thresholds passing leakage verification (LV < 10%); open markers indicate thresholds where measured leakage exceeds 10% (shown for reference but excluded from paper claims). Three of the four SFMs degrade monotonically with increasing OOD stringency but do not collapse to chance. dtSFM exhibits the inverse pattern — OOD retrieval (R@1 = 46.0% at 80% identity) exceeds in-distribution retrieval (R@1 = 27.7%) — consistent with the framework's prediction that OOD splits act as regularizers forcing the model toward the physical solution when the architecture implements the governing equation. crisprSFM is the one domain in this set whose binding rule (Watson–Crick complementarity) admits a competitive trivial sequence-similarity baseline (Hamming distance), which coincides with its late convergence and standard-DL gap in **a** and **b**. mhcSFM and mir-SFM are omitted from **c** because similarity-based splits are degenerate in both domains. All values are computed directly from the per-epoch training logs and pool-512 archival sources cited in `fig3_combined_data.csv`.
