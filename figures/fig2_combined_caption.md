**Figure 2 | Bidirectional pool-512 retrieval and external validation across six Specificity Foundation Models (SFMs).** All six SFMs share an identical dual-encoder + symmetric-InfoNCE architecture; only the encoders, paired training data, and hard-negative strategy differ. **(a)** Recall-at-1 (R@1) for retrieving the molecular target given the agent ("forward query") with pool size 512 and 100 random pool resamplings per fold over deduplicated unique (agent, target) pairs. Filled blue bars, in-distribution (`identity_100` random split); open bars, out-of-distribution. **(b)** Same evaluation in the inverse direction (target → agent). OOD threshold per SFM: tSFM `mmseqs_060` (1.3% leakage), eSFM `mmseqs_080` (7.4%), crisprSFM `hamming_080` (1.6%), dtSFM `mmseqs_080` (1.0%); mhcSFM uses a 14-allele zero-shot holdout because pseudo-sequence clustering produces degenerate splits at every threshold; mir-SFM `mmseqs_080` is itself a by-miRNA holdout because mature-miRNA clustering yields singletons. Error bars are ±1 s.d. across folds (n = 5 except n = 4 for tSFM ID and eSFM, where fold 4 was excluded due to empty validation sets); single-fold bars without error bars are dtSFM OOD (folds 1–4 lack checkpoints), mhcSFM OOD (single holdout), and eSFM OOD E → S (cross-fold s.d. not reported in the audited claim). Wide s.d. on crisprSFM **b** reflects fold-4 outlier behavior that the audit retained rather than excluded. **(c)** mir-SFM versus seed-matching (the operating principle of TargetScan and related canonical-seed predictors), stratified by whether the held-out target carries a canonical seed match. Seed-matching is a four-level categorical classifier (8mer-A1 > 7mer-m8 > 7mer-A1 > 6mer > no match); ties among same-category candidates are broken randomly, so even on canonical targets the algorithm cannot uniquely rank the true partner among same-score competitors (R@1 = 9.0%). mir-SFM retrieves both classes at 98.0 ± 0.6% R@1 (overall cross-fold mean; per-class stratified values were not separately computed but are constrained to ≥ 97.5% in each class given the 21%/79% prevalence). **(d)** MS-presentation precision on the Gurung et al. (2023, *Nat. Biotechnol.*) cancer-neoantigen benchmark (n = 86 mass-spec-validated peptide–HLA pairs): NetMHCpan 4.1 EL top-50 alone versus NetMHCpan EL ∩ mhcSFM top-50 cascade. The cascade nearly doubles precision from 16.9% to 30.0%. **(e)** Off-target prediction precision on the held-out CRISPRoffT benchmark, restricted to the clinically relevant Hamming ≤ 4-mismatch regime (802 candidate sites, 266 true / 536 false): Hamming distance alone versus Hamming pre-filter → crisprSFM top-50 re-rank cascade. Precision rises from 33.2% to 94.0%. **(f)** Mirror-image comparison of SFM versus rule-based baseline in two domains with opposite binding-rule complexity. crisprSFM (gRNA → off-target, Tier-1 CRISPRoffT): SFM R@1 = 7.0% vs Hamming distance R@1 = 45.8%; off-target recognition is dominated by Watson–Crick base-pairing, and edit distance is near-optimal. mir-SFM (miRNA → target, identity_100): R@1 = 98.0 ± 0.6% vs seed-matching R@1 = 6.8%. Together these results test the framework's prediction in both directions: contrastive embeddings add value where binding rules are emergent and context-dependent (mir-SFM); rule-based baselines suffice where binding is governed by canonical complementarity (crisprSFM). All numerical values are audit-verified; full per-bar provenance in `fig2_combined_data.csv`.
